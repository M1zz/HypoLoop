- **First comment** (제출 직후 본인이 첫 댓글로):

```
Author here. I've run challenge-based team education for years and kept
hitting the same wall: the team ships, but I can't tell what each
individual actually learned. The more rational the team, the more each
person sticks to what they're already good at.

HypoLoop flips the premise. Learning is the goal; the team deliverable
is the means. The engine is a hypothesis cycle: before you learn
anything, you write down your current (probably wrong) understanding,
a teammate asks "if this breaks, what will you learn?", then the team
project itself becomes the lab where you break it. What gets graded is
the delta between your starting and ending understanding — not the
deliverable.

The site has an 8-step playbook with templates and worked examples.
It's also available in Korean, Japanese, and Chinese. I'd love feedback,
especially from people who mentor or run onboarding.
```

### Reddit — r/ExperiencedDevs (멘토링 관점)

- **Title**: `How do you make sure individuals actually learn during team projects? I built a framework around "required failure"`

```
Long-time lurker, mentor at a team-project-based program. My recurring
frustration: the team ships fine, but individuals plateau — everyone
gravitates to what they're already good at (rationally!).

I ended up formalizing what worked into a framework called HypoLoop.
The core mechanic: before learning anything new, you write a hypothesis
from your current understanding ("I believe X works like this; doing Y
will reveal if I'm right"). A peer asks one question: "If this breaks,
what will you learn?" Then the team project itself is where you break it.
Retros measure the delta in understanding, not the deliverable.

For senior folks the rule "pick what you can't do" points at adjacent
domains (design, the market, accessibility) rather than basics.

Site with the full 8-step playbook: https://m1zz.github.io/HypoLoop/

How do others handle the "experts only do expert things" trap in
team settings?
```

### Reddit — r/learnprogramming (학습자 관점)

- **Title**: `Try writing your (wrong) hypothesis BEFORE you google — it changed how much I retain`

```
A technique from a learning framework I've been using (HypoLoop):
before you search, watch a tutorial, or ask an AI — write three lines
with zero information:

  "I currently understand X as ___.
   If I try Y, it will reveal whether I'm right.
   If this breaks, I will learn ___."

Being wrong is the point. When your prediction breaks against reality,
the gap is exactly what sticks. A prediction that never breaks means
you picked something too safe.

Full method (8 steps, free, no signup): https://m1zz.github.io/HypoLoop/
```
